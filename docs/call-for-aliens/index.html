Inter­galactic Receiver

Maybe this is the first device to receive alien transmissions. A curious object that looks like a vintage radio, or a computer from Severance. It has a weird antenna at the top, and it is connected to a flickering, low-resolution CRT television.

Mostly the screen shows static. But as you fiddle with the tuning knob, you soon discover that there are stations at certain frequencies. The transmissions are abstract procedural animations. They look like something created by alien minds: mathematical, geometric, but somehow also warm and compassionate. They are multi-dimensional streams of friendship and curiosity from outer space.


* This image shows the calibration of the Receiver's controls. The finished Receiver will be connected to a full-sized color TV, which will show either static or animations generated from code in real time.

Or is this art? Maybe the animations were coded up by creative sorcerers who practice the dark art of shader graphics, or Hydra video synthesis. That would make the Inter­galactic Receiver a collaborative art project, a platform for the work of several algorithmic artists.

Text of message:
Refracted propagation picked up by sublight sensors reveals that a new quantum entanglement device has been activated in the 3JNE-CCB system. The system's indigenous civilization has not previously been capable of intergalactic communication. Only a single device is in operation, and it is only capable of receiving quantum entanglements. At a pre-solarpunk stage of development it is to be expected that the operators do not yet possess the kind of laminar energy flows needed to spawn outgoing entaglement.

On past occasions the mistake has been made to broadcast a purely rationalistic message devoid of creativity, playfulness, art or compassion. We are therefore announcing this Open Call for all members of Generative Friendship and Galactic Compassion.

Please respond with existing or suggested shader art within the receiver's rudimentary computational bounds:

  • 720x576 pixel canvas at 50FPS; possibly an 80-pixel inivisible overscan on both sides
  • Executed by the VideoCore IV GPU of a Raspberry Pi 4B, and rendered over Composite Video on a cathode ray tube TV
  • OpenGL ES 3.1 is supported, i.e., GLSL ES 3.10, i.e., #version 310 es. This uses in/out instead of attribute and varying.
  • The shader will run in a headless environment (no window server). The renderer is a small, efficient, dependency-free app written in C++ on top of the DRM/GBM/EGL stack, drawing directly to the screen.
  • The sketch can be a fragment shader, or a vertex+fragment shader, and it can include frame-by-frame calculations on the CPU. The simpler the better, and it would be best if you can make do without interim framebuffers (but we can talk). In any case I'll be happy to do the bulk of porting your sketch to C++ if needed.
  • The sketch can also be Hydra! There are some limitations, but we can extract the shader code from most Hydra sketches and have them executed on the device's GPU directly.
  • The sketch can be interactive! The receiver discovered in the 3JNE-CCB system includes three linear inputs, which the native beings control by turning knobs on the device. One of these knobs is very noisy and imprecise, so that channel will be smoothly quantized to 4 or 8 values.
  • We will map our transmissions to a scale familiar in the system. It's called the FM band and goes from 88 to 108 MHz in local units.
  • We will side-load every station with author information. When the tuner is slightly off your station's frequency, we will overlay text in the system's native alphabet. You can present your name or alias, and any other authorship information you wish, in this side-loaded information overlay.
  • All software and hardware details of the system built on 3JNE-CCB are available here: github.com/gugray/InterGalacticReceiver.
